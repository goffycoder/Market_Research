# BOJ Analysis: March 30, 2026 to April 3, 2026

Latest local data available in this workspace runs through April 3, 2026. There are no later BOJ data files in the repo as of April 5, 2026.

For rough USD translation, this note uses USD/JPY around 159.67 per $1, matching the April 3, 2026 close shown in the historical USD/JPY data used for reference. Dollar figures are approximate.

## Executive View

The Bank of Japan is not signaling renewed easing.

It is signaling a slow, deliberate normalization path:

- keep the policy rate at 0.75% for now,
- keep shrinking excess liquidity gradually,
- keep buying JGBs at a reduced but still meaningful pace to avoid disorderly markets,
- watch wages and underlying inflation more than noisy headline CPI,
- and avoid tightening too fast while Middle East energy shocks and external policy risks could hurt growth.

The most important message across the files is this:

Headline inflation noise is not the BOJ's main guide. The BOJ is telling markets to focus on the wage-price cycle and underlying inflation, while accepting a continued reduction in the size of its balance sheet.

## What Changed In The Data

### 1. March liquidity was still shrinking in stock terms

From the March 2026 current-account and market-operations file:

- March 2026 month-end current account balances: 4,597,433 (100 million yen) = JPY 459.7 trillion, about $2.88 trillion.
- March 2026 month-end reserve balances: 4,210,409 (100 million yen) = JPY 421.0 trillion, about $2.64 trillion.
- FY2025 year-over-year drop in current account balances: 706,893 (100 million yen) = JPY 70.7 trillion, about $442.7 billion.
- FY2025 year-over-year drop in reserve balances: 569,222 (100 million yen) = JPY 56.9 trillion, about $356.5 billion.

Interpretation:

The BOJ is still draining the huge liquidity stock left from QE. The system is still very liquid, but it is meaningfully less liquid than a year earlier.

### 2. March flow data show a modest monthly drain in balances

From the same March 2026 market-operations file:

- March funds surplus in money markets: JPY 1.94 trillion, about $12.2 billion.
- March net change in current account balances: minus JPY 1.36 trillion, about minus $8.5 billion.

Interpretation:

Even though the monthly funding environment was not tight in a crisis sense, the end result was still a reduction in BOJ current-account balances. That is consistent with gradual normalization.

### 3. April is projected to be a big temporary liquidity-add month

From the April 2026 current-account projection file:

- April projected funds surplus: JPY 6.34 trillion, about $39.7 billion.
- Main stated driver of both shortage and surplus days: "Treasury funds and others."

Interpretation:

This does not mean the BOJ turned dovish in April. It mostly means fiscal flows are expected to inject cash temporarily. The repo files explicitly show that the biggest daily swings are expected to come mainly from treasury factors, not from a policy pivot.

### 4. JGB buying continues, but on a smaller and more controlled scale

From the March 31, 2026 quarterly purchase schedule:

- Planned monthly JGB purchases for April-June 2026: 27,050 (100 million yen) = JPY 2.705 trillion per month, about $16.9 billion per month.
- March 2026 outright JGB purchases in the monthly operations file: 27,440 (100 million yen) = JPY 2.744 trillion, about $17.2 billion.

Interpretation:

The BOJ is still in the market, but this is not aggressive QE-style balance-sheet expansion. It is more like controlled market smoothing while the overall policy stance normalizes.

### 5. BOJ JGB holdings fell sharply late in March

From the JGB-holdings files:

- March 19, 2026 total BOJ JGB holdings: 5,428,548 (100 million yen) = JPY 542.9 trillion.
- March 31, 2026 total BOJ JGB holdings: 5,276,805 (100 million yen) = JPY 527.7 trillion, about $3.30 trillion.
- March 19 to March 31 decline: 151,743 (100 million yen) = JPY 15.17 trillion, about $95.0 billion.

Largest declines from March 19 to March 31 were concentrated in:

- 5Y JGBs: minus JPY 5.94 trillion
- 10Y JGBs: minus JPY 7.70 trillion
- 20Y JGBs: minus JPY 1.66 trillion

Interpretation:

This is one of the clearest balance-sheet signals in the workspace. The BOJ is still purchasing bonds, but maturities and runoff are overtaking gross buying in parts of the curve. That is balance-sheet normalization in practice.

### 6. The BOJ still has a very large collateral and liquidity backstop

From the collateral file released April 2, 2026:

- Total collateral accepted face amount: JPY 149.9 trillion, about $939.1 billion.
- Total collateral accepted value: JPY 121.4 trillion, about $760.1 billion.
- Collateral value as a share of face amount: about 81%.

Largest collateral categories by face amount:

- Housing loan trust beneficial interests: JPY 48.0 trillion
- Loans on deeds to the government: JPY 28.2 trillion
- Government securities: JPY 26.2 trillion
- Municipal bonds: JPY 19.4 trillion

Interpretation:

Even while the BOJ is normalizing, it still has a very deep operational safety net. This reduces the risk that normalization turns into a market accident.

## What The BOJ Is Actually Trying To Say

The policy statement, summary of opinions, CPI note, output-gap update, and underlying-inflation review all point to the same message.

### Message 1. The BOJ still leans toward more tightening, not less

The March 19 monetary policy statement says:

- the overnight call rate should remain around 0.75% for now,
- real rates are still significantly low,
- and if the outlook is realized, the BOJ will continue to raise the policy rate and adjust monetary accommodation.

This is not neutral language. It is a tightening bias, just not an urgent one.

### Message 2. The BOJ thinks true inflation is near, but not safely above, 2%

The March 30 summary of opinions and the March 30 review on underlying inflation both stress:

- headline CPI is being distorted by rice, energy, and government support measures,
- underlying CPI is still the key concept,
- wages and prices are feeding each other more than before,
- but underlying inflation is not yet so firmly above 2% that the BOJ should rush.

In plain English:

The BOJ thinks inflation is close enough to target to keep normalizing, but not stable enough above target to slam on the brakes.

### Message 3. Oil and geopolitics are the main reason the BOJ paused instead of hiking in March

The summary of opinions repeatedly points to:

- Middle East tension,
- higher crude oil prices,
- risk to Japanese growth through terms of trade,
- and the possibility of stagflation if cost-push inflation persists while growth slows.

So the BOJ is balancing two opposing risks:

- if it waits too long, it may fall behind the curve and let inflation expectations rise,
- if it hikes too fast into an oil shock, it may damage growth.

That is why the tone is hawkish in direction but cautious in timing.

### Message 4. The BOJ wants the market to stop over-reading headline CPI prints

The March 26 CPI release is especially important.

The BOJ started publishing CPI estimates that exclude institutional factors such as:

- energy-cost relief measures,
- other government price policies,
- and other one-off distortions.

This is a communication choice, not just a statistical one.

It means the BOJ is telling markets:

"Do not treat every headline CPI move as policy-relevant. Look through distortions and judge the underlying trend."

### Message 5. The BOJ is broadening the dashboard beyond CPI

The March 26 output-gap update says the BOJ is also publishing labor-market indicators alongside the output gap because labor tightness matters more for wages and prices now.

That matters because the BOJ is increasingly focused on:

- labor shortages,
- wage pass-through,
- pricing power in services,
- and medium-term inflation expectations.

This is another sign that the BOJ is moving away from a simple headline-CPI framework toward a broader "is the wage-price cycle self-sustaining?" framework.

## The Crux Behind The Spreadsheet Moves

The crux is not that the BOJ is easing or tightening in one dramatic step.

The crux is that the BOJ is trying to engineer a very specific combination:

- positive policy rates,
- a still-orderly JGB market,
- a smaller balance sheet over time,
- and inflation that stays near 2% because of domestic wage-price dynamics rather than imported cost shocks.

That explains why the spreadsheets look mixed:

- current account balances are still huge, but down sharply year over year,
- JGB purchases continue, but total JGB holdings can still fall,
- April can show a projected liquidity surplus, but that can be mainly fiscal timing,
- and official communication can sound hawkish while the actual meeting result is unchanged.

Those are not contradictions. They are exactly what a cautious exit from ultra-easy policy looks like.

## Bottom Line In Market Terms

If I compress all of the files into one market message, it is this:

The BOJ is still on a normalization path, but it wants to normalize slowly enough to avoid breaking growth or the bond market. It is using communication to separate underlying inflation from noisy headline inflation, while allowing excess liquidity and bond holdings to keep trending lower over time.
